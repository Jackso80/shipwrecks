“Life at Sea in Merchant Service in the 1870s.” Dawlish Chronicles, 7 Apr. 2017,

The number of ships grew profoundly in the latter half of the 19th century to meet the demands of globalization. This increase in production often led to a decrease in the care of ships being built, including the integrity of ships. In the year 1873-1874, 411 vessels sank off of the British coast and around 500 lives were lost as well. What was responsible for this was not, in fact, whether or bad seamanship, but instead “ships frequently broke up or fell apart for the simple reason that they were already rotten and worn-out.” Indeed safety regulations were set amid the 1830s. In fact, “ Standards for structural integrity and for limits on loading had indeed been established as early as 1835 by Lloyd’s Register and compliance was a prerequisite for insurance by reputable entities associated with Lloyds.”

“Ships and Boats: 1840-1950” English Heritage, 2012

This article explains various ship designs, evolution and adaptation of ship designs, and how and what events inspired them to take place during various time periods and used for specific time periods. For example, the Victorian ship designs were used from 1837 to 1901. This then transitioned to the Early Twentieth Century design used from 1901 to 1932. Shipbuilding technologies increase further into the end of the 1830s implementing iron as shipbuilding material and incorporating screw propeller-driven, which “in this case by a direct-acting engine which applied power directly to the crankshaft.” Experimentation with the screw and paddle propulsion in 1845 culminated in a towing contest between the screw-driven HMS Rattler and the paddle frigate. The changeover from paddle powered to screw propulsion occurred and changed gradually over 20 years from the 1840s. In fact, the Cunard Company continued to order paddle streamers up through the 1860s. Britain was able to manufacture engines at lower costs than other countries over the course of the 19th century due to an abundance of many metal items, this contributed to the development of marine steam engines. As engines became more complex, they started being classified solely according to their cylinder technology, or how many cylinders are in the engine. By 1834, implementations of surface condensers, iron for shipbuilding, and screw propellers were significant technological advancements in ship design. Another side note, during this time there was a war called the Crimean War (October 1853 to February 1856), this war was fought to “gain influence over the territories of the declining Ottoman Empire, is considered to be one of the first modern wars because of the tactical use of new technology like the electric telegraph and railways.” What this war showed and inspired for ship designers alike was that pure wooden sail ships were highly vulnerable to fire from (at the time) modern based artillery.
